GitLab founder Sid Sijbrandij's detailed personal account of treating osteosarcoma (bone cancer in the T5 vertebrae) after standard-of-care options were exhausted. After relapse following initial treatment, Sid took radical ownership of his own survival: assembling a personal medical team, running maximal diagnostics as frequently as possible, using AI tools for deep research, and developing a therapeutic ladder using repurposed drugs and personalized medicine. His cancer is currently in remission.

The central philosophy is "we don't need to know what cured you" — a tolerance for epistemic uncertainty that standard oncology often cannot accommodate institutionally. Sid pursued every diagnostic and therapeutic avenue simultaneously rather than waiting for proof, treating his own survival as an optimization problem he had to run himself.

## Key Takeaways

- Maximal diagnostics (frequent, broad testing) provides more signal than waiting for clinical thresholds
- Patient-driven AI research can surface repurposed drugs and personalized medicine options outside standard protocols
- Building your own multidisciplinary medical team gives access to specialists who can collaborate outside standard referral paths
- Raw data is available for download — unusually transparent for personal medical documentation
- The framing "it became my own job to keep myself alive" captures the mindset shift required when standard care has nothing left to offer
