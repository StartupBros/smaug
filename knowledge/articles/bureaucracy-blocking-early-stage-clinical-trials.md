## Summary

A comprehensive analysis of how unnecessary bureaucratic red tape has made early-stage clinical trials (small-n trials) prohibitively expensive and time-consuming, preventing patients from accessing experimental treatments. The article uses the story of an Australian entrepreneur who treated his dog's cancer with a personalized mRNA vaccine as a starting point, then shows how similar regulatory barriers block life-saving treatments for humans, even when patients are willing to self-fund experimentation.

## Key Takeaways

- **Regulatory bloat**: Creating red tape for a single dog trial took 3 months of nightly documentation work, while designing the vaccine itself took less time. The US has even more complex requirements.

- **Inequitable access**: Only people with extraordinary resources (like GitLab founder Sid Sijbrandij) can navigate the system to access experimental therapies. Most patients cannot.

- **Unnecessary costs**: Clinical trials in Australia cost 2.5-3x less than in the US with no increase in safety events—proving unnecessary bureaucracy adds cost without improving outcomes.

- **Stagnation parallel**: Just as bans on human dissection stagnated medical science for centuries, current regulatory barriers may be preventing AI-driven and personalized medicine breakthroughs from reaching patients.

- **AI enables innovation but not access**: AI can design new therapies far faster than before, but regulatory barriers prevent testing them in humans, negating the speed advantage.

- **Early-stage trials are learning, not just validation**: Small-n trials serve a crucial discovery function (learning how therapies work in humans), not just regulatory checkpoints.

- **Right-to-try doesn't work in practice**: While laws exist, pharmaceutical companies rarely provide experimental drugs outside formal trials, making early-stage trials the only practical path.

- **Ultra-rare diseases need flexibility**: Personalized medicine and treatments for rare diseases don't fit traditional large-scale trial models and require adaptive, small-n designs.

## Key Arguments

The article argues for reforming early-stage clinical trial regulations specifically:
- Lower barriers to small-n exploratory trials (1-few patients testing experimental therapies)
- Maintain safety standards while reducing administrative burden
- Enable personalized medicine and ultra-rare disease treatments
- Create a "right-to-try" that actually functions in practice
- Learn from Australia's more efficient trial system
