Dan Koe's framework for developing what he calls "5-dimensional thinking" — a progression from one-dimensional dogmatic thinking to a multi-axis model integrating lines (domains of knowledge), levels (depth of understanding within domains), altitude (cross-domain average), four perspectives on reality (inner/outer × individual/collective), and finally time/history as the fifth dimension. The core argument is that intelligence measured by domain depth alone ("knowing") is less valuable than the vertical capacity to continue thinking past the point where most people get defensive and stop.

## Key Takeaways

- **Stupid thinking is stopping thinking**: The defining feature of low-quality thinking isn't ignorance but defensive closure — latching onto an answer and defending it rather than continuing to explore.
- **Lines vs. Levels vs. Altitude**: Lines = horizontal domain knowledge; Levels = depth of understanding within any domain (conformist → individualist → synthesist → generative); Altitude = your average level across all lines. Specialists get stuck because they can't "put points" into other domains needed to unlock higher levels in their primary one.
- **Four dimensions of reality**: Every problem can be examined through individual-inner (psychology), individual-outer (behavior/body), collective-inner (culture/ideology), collective-outer (systems/institutions). Genuinely strategic thinking toggles between all four rather than reducing to one.
- **The 5th dimension is time**: Understanding evolutionary *patterns* in history (not memorizing events) gives foresight. The master pattern is "transcend and include" — each higher level contains the lower rather than replacing it.
- **Identity as the primary obstacle**: People stop thinking when an idea threatens their identity. Noticing the moment of defensiveness and choosing to stay open is the single highest-leverage practice.
