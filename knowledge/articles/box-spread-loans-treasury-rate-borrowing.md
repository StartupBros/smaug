A detailed explanation of box spread loans — a four-legged options strategy using European-style options (typically SPX or XSP) that creates a synthetic loan at near-Treasury rates. The basic structure: sell a lower-strike call, buy a higher-strike call, buy a lower-strike put, sell a higher-strike put, all with the same expiration. The net credit received equals the loan proceeds. At January 2026 rates, this allows borrowing at ~4% or less — 100bps better than margin loans, mortgages, or most other financing channels.

A built-in tax advantage makes the effective rate even lower: the "interest" paid manifests as a capital loss under Section 1256, classified as 60% long-term / 40% short-term capital loss, deductible against portfolio gains. In high tax brackets the true borrowing cost can reach ~3%. The article also covers the inverse strategy via the BOXX ETF, which earns treasury-like yield with capital gains tax treatment instead of ordinary income. Key risks include execution complexity (requires portfolio margin account, specific options approval level), margin call risk if the position's size exceeds ~30% of account value, and liquidity risk on very long-dated positions.

## Key Takeaways

- Box spreads on SPX/XSP create synthetic loans at ~4% or less — near Treasury rates
- Requires 4 legs: sell lower-strike call, buy higher-strike call, buy lower-strike put, sell higher-strike put
- Interest is captured as a Section 1256 capital loss (60% LT / 40% ST) — deductible against portfolio gains
- Effective rate in high tax brackets can reach ~3% after the tax benefit
- Requires portfolio margin account and specific options approval level; strongly recommended to use a professional
- BOXX ETF is the inverse — earns treasury-like yield taxed as capital gains instead of ordinary income
- Keep borrowing under 30% of account value to avoid margin call risk from rate fluctuations
