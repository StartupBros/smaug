Sparked by the viral story of Paul Conyngham designing a personalized mRNA vaccine for his dog Rosie using AI tools, Teslo argues that small early-stage clinical trials have become unnecessarily expensive and bureaucratic — a bottleneck that will prevent AI from delivering on its promise of accelerating medical progress. She draws parallels to Sid Sijbrandij (GitLab co-founder who self-funded his way through osteosarcoma relapse) and Jake Seliger (writer who died unable to access trials he couldn't afford to navigate). Australia runs equivalent trials 2.5–3x cheaper than the US with no increase in safety events. Teslo co-founded the Clinical Trial Abundance initiative in 2024 to push for reform.

## Key Takeaways
- Early-stage small-n trials are the "right to try" in practice — not the law, which rarely works
- Personalized medicine and ultra-rare diseases can't scale to large Phase III trial formats
- Early trials are learning tools, not just regulatory checkpoints — essential for closing clinic-in-the-loop feedback
- AI will not automatically accelerate drug discovery if the clinical trial bottleneck remains
- Australia already proves cheaper/faster trials are possible without safety tradeoffs
- The system effectively reserves experimental access to those with billionaire-level resources to navigate it
