Josh Miller (founder of The Browser Company / Arc) describes the concrete organizational changes Claude Code has enabled at his company, offering one of the most specific accounts of how an established product team is adapting to AI-native workflows. Designers are submitting PRs; non-engineers are prototyping independently; engineers are running more experimental bets in parallel without derailing core work. Miller's framing is that it's like the whole team got personal e-bikes to explore and build.

The piece draws an explicit analogy to mobile-native adoption in the early 2010s: teams that don't go Claude Code-native now will fall behind the way companies that didn't go mobile-first did. Miller describes three concrete organizational responses: paying a premium for exceptional talent native to Claude Code workflows; treating engineers like artists (optimizing for flow state), and doing fewer things with more depth and more risk tolerance. The article announces a new "Design Producer" role to coordinate product work in this new paradigm.

## Key Takeaways

- Real-world evidence of cross-functional capability expansion: designers writing code, non-engineers prototyping, engineers running more experiments
- The "Claude Code-native" framing is the Browser Company's equivalent of "mobile-native" — a structural shift in how software is conceived and built
- Organizational response requires re-thinking compensation, process design, and role definitions — not just adding a new tool
- Boris Cherny (Claude Code creator at Anthropic) reportedly runs 5-10 instances in parallel as a working pattern, which Miller cites as signal of what's coming
- The "Design Producer" role is a novel hybrid emerging from this transition: coordinating creative/product work across design, engineering, and AI-assisted development
