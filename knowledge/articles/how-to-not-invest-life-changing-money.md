Ankur Nagpal recounts his experience managing the proceeds from selling his startup in April 2020 — starting with zero personal finance knowledge and receiving a life-changing wire transfer at 31. He split the proceeds roughly 50/50 between Goldman Sachs private wealth management and his own "fun money" portfolio. After five years, Goldman's portfolio returned ~10% IRR (dragged by fixed income and underperforming small cap funds), while his own grew ~80% or ~14% IRR — still trailing the S&P 500's 18% annual return over the same period. The biggest pre-sale win was aggressive QSBS structuring (using a CRUT, a Dynasty Trust, and personal holdings to triple the $10M exclusion), which was worth more than all his investing alpha combined. Goldman's AUM fees eroded compounding meaningfully, and he found no meaningful investing edge in their private deal flow. Going forward he plans to direct-index himself and stop paying AUM fees.

## Key Takeaways

- QSBS tax planning (multiplying the exclusion via trusts) was the single highest-leverage move, dwarfing any investment returns
- Neither Goldman Sachs private wealth management nor his own stock-picking beat a simple S&P 500 index fund over five years
- AUM fees on large portfolios compound negatively — paying ~100bps on a large base destroys significant terminal wealth
- Individual stock picking underperformed badly during the 2021–2022 correction; indexing captures winners like Nvidia automatically
- Direct indexing for tax-loss harvesting is the preferred approach for deploying new capital going forward
- JP Morgan Private Bank praised as a genuinely free and useful banking product for those who qualify
