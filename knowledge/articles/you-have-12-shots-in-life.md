A framework for thinking about lifetime impact using the concept of "shots" — major multi-year projects or bets you can realistically make in a working life. The math: ~50 working years, ~4 years per major shot, equals roughly 12 shots total. Not all shots are equal, and the author draws on the Chinese concept of 天地人 (heaven/earth/person) to rank the forces that shape success: timing first, then environment/positioning, then the team.

The core insight is that results follow a power law — 20% of your shots produce 80% of your impact, and you only need to be right once out of 12 to achieve extraordinary things. This reframes the question from "how do I not fail?" to "how do I position myself for a shot that has real momentum?" The author distinguishes "G-force" opportunities (where energy compounds without pushing) from forced or desperate bets, and argues that the space between major shots — small experiments, relationship-building, positioning — matters as much as the shots themselves.

## Key Takeaways

- Timing (天) is the dominant force; a mediocre team catching the right wave beats a brilliant team solving the wrong problem
- You can't time the market intellectually — look for intuitive pull and observable momentum, not intellectual conviction
- Environment/geography (地) is the one force you can actively choose; position yourself where waves are forming
- The "red paperclip" principle: you can trade up from wherever you are through bias toward action and small experiments
- Evaluate shots on asymmetric upside, not just success probability — good shots "fail upward" by expanding capabilities and relationships
- Stop thinking in jobs; think in shots that each position you for bigger future shots
